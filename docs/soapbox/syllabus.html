How to write a syllabus and run a class: my experience as a TA

The title is a bit misleading because it also includes experience as a student. But in any case, there are many practices that I observe in the classes I have participated in. Some that I appreciate, and some that I do not appreciate.

Small aside, but you should type your syllabi in LaTeX. In general, you should type just about anything that you don't expect to have students edit [or fill out] in LaTeX. (Alternatively, give students a .tex file.) It looks nicer. If you don't know how to use LaTeX, you should learn how to use LaTeX. Overleaf is a wonderful website to use.

Basic information

Include your name, email, office, teaching team (and contact information), when and where your lecture takes place, course number, etc. Basically, the details on a class search page, surely you can't mess that up :)

About the Course

Use this section to describe what the course is about. To get a start, consider copying the class description verbatim, then elaborate upon the specific topics to be taught. You should also list the prerequisites and teach with the expectation that students know the prerequisite information. (For example, if I was teaching a class on computer architecture, and computer assembly language was a prerequisite, I am not going to spend more than five minutes on that stuff.)

Course Schedule

A course schedule should include the following:

  • A "calendar" that describes the topics to be discussed for each day
  • A list of assignments to be handed out, including when they will be released and when they are due. If you end up changing a date, let your students and teaching team know in advance!
  • Exam dates. If any of your exam dates change, similar to the point above, don't leave anyone in the dark.

Class-Related Communication

Student-to-Student Communication

In the year of our lord 2026, if you're not using Discord, you're already falling behind. I find that Discord works so much better for getting students to collaborate and learn with each other. Slack doesn't give me notifications when I need them, and Ed Discussion or Canvas discussion boards... don't get me started on those.

Also, don't leave your TAs to handle all of the communication, or the whole class for that matter. What the fuck. (I'm counting this as student-to-student communication because TAs are students.)

Instructor (or Teaching Team) to Student Communication

Canvas announcements and emails are a mainstream (and usually pretty good) way to handle this matter of communication. Well, assuming Canvas works, we just had two incidents this past year: AWS crapping itself and script kiddies conquering the world.

Discord is still a pretty good option to use. Well, if the instructor wants to keep Discord for smaller matters, then the instructor can choose not to join their class Discord. Just have TAs monitor that part.

You should always have email open. Here is my example of an email policy you should use:

Should you need to email the instructor of a matter related to the course, 
send an email that includes the course number and a brief description in your subject. For example:
    
CSE 360: Unclear wording on Project Phase 2 
    
Each email must be carbon-copied (CCed) to the instructors and ALL TAs (unless there is a compelling reason not to). 
For grading-related questions, the grader(s) must also be CCed. 
Emails that do not follow the aforementioned guideline will NOT be returned.

Once again, don't leave your TAs to handle all of the communication, or the whole class for that matter. Again, what the fuck.

Communication within the Teaching Team

Agree on something easy to use, such as SMS, Slack, or Discord. (I always prefer Discord for these matters.) Email is a bit ehhhh to use because you have to write a salutation, type out formal sentences, and sign off. So pedantic, but I don't mind too much personally.

Grading

Attendance

You should have students attend your class. Hold in-class activities at the end of each class to make sure that students are able to engage with the material. (Also present in a way that makes students able to understand, while encouraging them to be inquisitive!)

Projects

Self-explanatory, I hope. If it's a coding project, make sure that your students are learning and not lobotomising themselves using an LLM.

Make sure you, the instructor, know what the projects are supposed to do. Of course, you should also be able to complete the project and answer questions on your own, not defer every single thing to TAs. What the fuck.

Exams

Self-explanatory for the most part. Have your teaching team look through the exams to make sure that they are completable. If your TA says you're wrong, you should show them your work. Exams are quite necessary in this day and age where students are more inclined to lobotomise themselves with an LLM. Take an iron-fisted approach, don't let your students cheat, but be encouraging. Acknowledge that most of them are trying their best, and that you recognise that.

Regrade policies

We all make mistakes. Have some clear grading criteria so that students are able to see what they got wrong, especially for free-response questions. However, there is always the possibility that students may abuse regrading just to try haggling for a better grade. The policies I write will generally include:

  • A statement that acknowledges that you (or your teaching team) may have made a mistake
  • How to regrade: via email? via Gradescope?
  • Conditions for a regrade: Clearly explain why your answer is correct (whether it is a grader error, or grading criteria error)
  • Regrade requests that present false information (such as claiming an answer to be correct when it is not and not explaining why) will result in forfeiture of ALL points earned for the question. Including an explanation helps address potential misunderstandings. Presentation of false information to request a grade improvement without good reason (a good reason would include an honest misunderstanding) is never appropriate, such cases may constitute an academic integrity violation.
  • Give one week for grading appeals.
  • State that a student's score can go up, go down, or remain the same.
  • If your students disagree with the grader, the instructor should be reliable for them to ask about that. What the fuck.

Grade scale

As a general rule of thumb, 90% is an A. Not an A-. You don't have to use A-; an A- is less than an A. (Except in nursing; they use different rules there.)

University Policy

This includes academic integrity as a rule of thumb, threatening behaviour, mandated reporting, etc. Follow the general guidelines.

Academic Integrity

Make sure your students know what they can or cannot do. Especially with generative AI.

Disability Accommodations

As someone with ADHD, I get extra time on my exam due to difficulty concentrating, especially when a lot of writing takes place. Please make your students aware! (The testing center has cameras everywhere, so don't worry about them cheating; they will certainly get caught if they try anything funny.)
